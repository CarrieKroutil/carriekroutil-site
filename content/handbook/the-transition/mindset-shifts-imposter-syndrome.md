This deep-dive is still in progress, but the central shift is worth stating plainly now: as an engineer your value came from knowing the answer; as a manager it comes from helping other people find theirs. That trade — from being the smartest person in the room to making the room smarter — is also where impostor syndrome moves in, because your wins become quieter and harder to point at. This topic is about navigating that identity change without losing your footing.

What the full version will cover:

- Letting go of "expert" as your primary source of worth
- Recognizing the new, less visible signals that you're doing the job well
- Why impostor feelings spike right after a transition (and aren't a verdict)
- Concrete habits for steadying yourself on the hard days

{{< protip >}}
When the impostor voice gets loud, I look at my team instead of myself. Are they unblocked? Growing? Shipping things they're proud of? Those are my deliverables now — and on the days I can't feel my own value, my team's progress is the honest scoreboard.
{{< /protip >}}
